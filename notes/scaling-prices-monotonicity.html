Scaling of Price's Monotonicity Formula

Demo note showing MathJax rendering inside the new layout.

Price's monotonicity formula is one of the cleanest ways to package how the area ratio of a minimal submanifold reacts to rescaling. For a minimal surface $\Sigma \subset \mathbb{R}^{n}$ passing through the origin, the classical statement is $$\Theta_{\Sigma}(r) = \frac{\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma \cap B_r(0))}{\pi r^2}$$ which is nondecreasing in $r$. The numerator scales like $r^{2}$ while the denominator normalizes by the Euclidean area of the ball of radius $r$.

Rescaling the Domain

Suppose we consider the dilation $D_{\lambda}(x) = \lambda x$ with $\lambda > 0$. The pushforward surface $D_{\lambda}(\Sigma)$ remains minimal, and its density ratio satisfies

$$\Theta_{D_{\lambda}(\Sigma)}(r) = \Theta_{\Sigma}(\lambda r).$$

Consequently, the monotonicity can be restated in terms of the rescaled radius $\rho = \lambda r$, making it transparent how blow-ups at a point convert the formula into a statement about tangent cones. For sequences $\lambda_j \to 0$, the quantities $\Theta_{\Sigma}(\lambda_j r)$ converge to the density of the limiting cone.

Energy Perspective

Another useful presentation comes from viewing the numerator as an integral involving the mean curvature vector $H$: $$\frac{d}{dr}\Theta_{\Sigma}(r) = \frac{2}{r^{3}}\int_{\Sigma \cap B_r(0)} |(x^{\perp})|^{2} \, d\operatorname{Area}.$$ The perpendicular projection $x^{\perp}$ scales quadratically with $r$, so the derivative remains nonnegative. This provides a numerical measure of how far the surface deviates from being conical on each scale.

Summary

The monotonicity of $\Theta_{\Sigma}(r)$ is therefore stable under rescalings, and the structure of the derivative shows how curvature concentration affects the limit. This page demonstrates how MathJax renders inline expressions like $x^{\perp}$ and display equations inside the site's typography.
